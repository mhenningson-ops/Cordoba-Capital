The Problem

The Tax Code Has a Strategy Built Into It.
Most High Earners Never Use It.

If you're earning $200,000 or more in W-2 income, you're likely paying the IRS between 37–42 cents of every dollar you make. There's a legal, IRS-recognized strategy that eliminates that liability — and almost no one in your income bracket knows it exists.

01

Passive Loss Rules Lock You Out

Standard rental properties generate passive losses that can only offset passive income — not your W-2 salary. So traditional real estate investing doesn't help your tax bill.

02

Short-Term Rentals Are Different

Under IRC §469, STRs with an average guest stay of 7 days or fewer are classified as non-passive — meaning losses flow directly against your W-2 income with no cap.

03

Bonus Depreciation Makes It Work

A cost segregation study identifies personal property inside your STR that qualifies for 100% bonus depreciation in year one — creating a large paper loss that offsets your income immediately.

The Numbers

What This Actually Looks Like for You

On a $600,000 short-term rental property, a cost segregation study typically identifies 25–35% as personal property eligible for accelerated depreciation. With 100% bonus depreciation, that creates a significant paper loss in year one — and real cash back in your pocket.

STRs are classified differently — they're treated as non-passive by default when average guest stays are 7 days or fewer and you materially participate, meaning losses flow directly against your W-2 income regardless of your profession.
Material participation means you're actively involved in the operation of the property — not just passively investing. The IRS has several tests, and the most commonly used is spending at least 100 hours on the activity and more hours than any other individual. For an STR with a professional property manager, this is typically achievable. Your CPA will help you document this properly each year.
